This skill guides salary negotiation: understanding offers, researching market rates, and negotiating with confidence and data.

Negotiation is normal. Companies expect it. Starting negotiations early and negotiating properly can add $100k+ over your career.

## Understanding the Offer Package

An offer is more than base salary. Understand all the components:

### Base Salary
- The annual amount paid in regular paychecks
- Most transparent, non-negotiable once finalized (generally)

### Bonus
- Typically 10-30% of base salary for individual contributors
- 30-100%+ for leadership roles
- Usually tied to company performance or personal metrics
- Annual, paid once/twice a year

### Equity (Stock Options or RSUs)
- Long-term incentive; you own part of the company
- For public companies: Restricted Stock Units (RSUs)
- For private companies: Stock options (right to buy shares at exercise price)
- Vests over 4 years (typically 1-year cliff, then monthly/quarterly vesting)
- Value depends on company success (could be worth a lot, or nothing)

**Example:** $150k base, $50k bonus, $200k/4 years equity
- Year 1 cash: $150k + $50k = $200k + $50k equity (cliff) = $250k total value
- Year 4 cash: $200k total + $200k equity (fully vested) = $400k total

### Signing Bonus
- Upfront bonus when you sign
- Often used to offset equity vesting in new role
- Helps offset loss of unvested equity at old company

### Benefits
- Health insurance (medical, dental, vision)
- 401(k) matching (some companies match up to 6%)
- PTO / vacation days
- Remote work allowance, equipment
- Parental leave, education benefits
- Other perks (gym, commuter benefits, meals, etc.)

**Estimating value:**
- Health insurance: ~$10-15k/year
- 401(k) match (6%): ~$9k/year at $150k salary
- Other benefits: ~$5-10k/year

## Researching Market Rates

Know what "fair market" is before negotiating.

### Data Sources

**Levels.fyi**
- Search by company, role, level
- Crowd-sourced data from actual employees
- Shows base, bonus, equity, location
- Usually most accurate for tech companies

**Blind** (anonymous)
- Tech worker community, discuss salary anonymously
- Search for your company/role/level
- More recent than Levels sometimes
- More variability; people may exaggerate

**Glassdoor**
- Public-facing, searchable
- Less reliable (self-reported, sometimes outdated)
- Useful as a sanity check, not primary source

**Personal Network**
- Ask people at target companies what they make
- "What's the range for a [role] at [company]?"
- Most accurate but requires trust
- People often hesitate to share; ask with context ("I'm interviewing, trying to understand market rate")

### Location Adjustment

Salary varies by location:
- SF Bay Area: +30-50% vs national average
- NYC: +20-40%
- Seattle, Boston: +15-25%
- Rest of US: -10-20% vs SF
- Remote companies: Often pay market rate for your location, not company HQ location

If you're in a low-cost area and company is in SF, don't assume SF salary. Clarify their practice.

## Offer Evaluation Framework

When you get an offer, evaluate it:

**Step 1: Compare to market**
- Total compensation (base + bonus + equity value)
- Is it 25th percentile? 50th? 75th?
- At market range or below?

**Step 2: Compare to other offers (if you have them)**
- Is this offer better than alternatives?
- Which offer aligns with your priorities?

**Step 3: Assess risk/reward**
- Startup with equity: High risk, high potential reward
- Established company: Stable, predictable equity value
- Private company: Equity might be worth nothing

**Step 4: Assess role/growth**
- Does the role help you grow?
- Is the team/company solving interesting problems?
- Will this role open doors for next opportunity?

**Example evaluation:**
> Offer A: $200k base, $50k bonus, $300k equity (4 years), Series B startup
> - Total first year cash: $250k
> - Total potential (if equity worth $200 in 4 years): $600k spread over 4 years
> - Risk: Startup could fail, equity worthless
> - Growth: Scaling phase, lots of learning opportunity
>
> Offer B: $180k base, $50k bonus, $200k equity (4 years), stable company
> - Total first year cash: $230k
> - Total potential (equity likely worth 80-90% of grant): ~$350k spread over 4 years
> - Risk: Low, but stable pay is lower
> - Growth: Solid, but less cutting-edge
>
> Decision: If I want startup experience and can handle risk, Offer A. If I want stability and predictable income, Offer B.

## Negotiation Strategy

Negotiation is a conversation, not confrontation. Goal: Find a number both parties feel good about.

### Timing
**Best time to negotiate:** After offer is made, before you sign

**Avoid:**
- Negotiating before offer (might kill interest)
- Negotiating 2 weeks after signing (too late, they'll say it's locked)

### How to Start

**Option 1: Email**
> Hi [name],
>
> Thanks for the offer for [role]. I'm excited about the opportunity and the team. Before we finalize, I wanted to see if there's flexibility on the package.
>
> Based on my research and experience, I was expecting something closer to [X]. Could we discuss adjusting [base/bonus/equity]? Happy to chat more about this.
>
> Looking forward to finding an arrangement that works for both of us.

**Option 2: Call (often better for nuance)**
> "Hey [name], I got the offer and I'm excited about it. Before I sign, I wanted to talk through the comp. Based on market research and my background, I was thinking more in the [X] range. Is there flexibility there?"

### Anchoring

**Strategy:** Make the first number in the conversation, but make it reasonable.
- Too low: You lose money
- Too high: They shut down negotiation
- Just-right: 10-20% above their offer, or at 75th percentile of market

**Example:**
- Their offer: $180k base
- Market range: $160-220k for your level/company
- Your anchor: $200-210k

**Why:** Anchors the conversation upward. Even if they counter at $190k, that's better than you asking and they saying "best we can do is $180k."

### Negotiation Levers

You can negotiate:
1. **Base salary:** Easiest to negotiate
2. **Bonus:** Sometimes flexible
3. **Equity:** Sometimes flexible, especially for startup (they might not have allocated all budget)
4. **Signing bonus:** Often available (especially if you're losing equity elsewhere)
5. **Vacation days, remote work, title:** Sometimes negotiable

**Strategy:**
- Lead with base (biggest lever)
- If they say base is fixed, ask about signing bonus or equity
- Don't ask for everything at once ("I want X base, Y equity, Z signing bonus")
- Pick the 1-2 most important to you

### Counter-Offer Responses

**If they say "base is fixed":**
> "I understand. Is there flexibility on the signing bonus or equity to bridge the gap?"

**If they ask "what number would make you happy?":**
> "Based on market research and my experience, [X] would feel right to me. What flexibility do you have?"

(Don't be the first to name a number lower than your anchor.)

**If they counter below your ask:**
> "I appreciate the counter. [New offer] is closer, but I was hoping for [original ask]. Can we land at [split the difference]?"

**If they say "we can't move further":**
> Then you decide: Is the offer good enough? If yes, accept. If no, you can walk (risky, but honest).

## Special Cases

### Multiple Offers
If you have competing offers, you can leverage them:
> "I have another offer at [amount]. Your offer is strong, but [competitor] offered [number]. Is there flexibility to match?"

Be honest, but professional. They know you might have other offers.

### Offer with Long Deadline
If they give you 24-48 hours:
> "I appreciate the timeline. I need a bit more time to evaluate properly. Could we extend to [X days]?"

Most companies will give you a week. If they won't, that's a red flag about their culture.

### Exploding Offers
Offers that expire in 24 hours (sometimes from startups):
- Red flag. Good companies don't pressure you into signing in 24 hours.
- If you're interested, negotiate quickly.
- If you're not sure, walk away (lots of other companies out there).

## After You Negotiate

**If they accept your counter:**
- Congratulations. Sign and celebrate.

**If they decline:**
- Decide: Is the original offer good enough?
- If yes: Sign and commit.
- If no: Politely decline and keep interviewing elsewhere.

**Don't:**
- Negotiate in bad faith and then reject the offer anyway (burns bridges)
- Keep asking for more after they've said final offer
- Start a new job angry about comp (resentment kills motivation)

## Throughout Negotiation

Keep it professional and collaborative:
- Assume good intent (they're not trying to screw you)
- Use data (market research, not feelings)
- Frame as "finding the right mutual fit," not "I need more money"
- Stay curious: "What flexibility do you have?" (not "Give me more")
- Be willing to say no (sometimes the best negotiation tactic is walking away)
